Famous People, Landmarks & History

Leonardo da Vinci

Leonardo da Vinci was a famous Italian Renaissance artist, known for his paintings, drawings, and notes on various subjects. He is considered a genius and one of the greatest painters in history, and his works, including the Mona Lisa and The Last Supper, are iconic. Da Vinci also made numerous technological inventions, including flying machines and concentrated solar power, but most were not feasible at the time. He made significant discoveries in various fields, but did not publish them and had little impact on science.

Donatello

Donatello was a renowned Florentine sculptor of the Renaissance who studied classical sculpture and used it to create a new Renaissance style. He traveled to other cities, where he worked on commissions and taught others, and his techniques were introduced to other parts of Italy. With financial support from Cosimo de Medici, Donatello created the first freestanding male nude sculpture since antiquity, the famous statue of David. He worked with various materials, including stone, bronze, wood, clay, stucco, and wax, and had several assistants. Donatello is known for his statues in the round, but he also created shallow bas-reliefs for more minor works and large architectural reliefs.

Michelangelo

Michelangelo was a prominent Italian Renaissance artist known for his sculptures, paintings, architecture, and poetry. He was inspired by classical models and significantly impacted Western art. Michelangelo is considered a quintessential Renaissance man, along with his contemporary Leonardo da Vinci. He is one of the most well-documented artists of the 16th century, and his biographers praised him as the most accomplished artist of his time. Michelangelo achieved fame early with his sculptures of Pietà and David and is known for his influential frescoes on the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel in Rome and on the altar wall of The Last Judgment. He also designed the Laurentian Library and was the architect of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome. Michelangelo's work was admired for its ability to evoke a sense of awe in viewers, and his style influenced the rise of Mannerism.

Raphael

Raphael was a famous Italian painter and architect during the High Renaissance. He is known for the clarity and ease of composition in his work, as well as his depiction of the Neoplatonic ideal of human grandeur. Along with Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo, Raphael is considered one of the three great masters of the period. He had a productive career, running a large workshop and completing a significant amount of work before his early death at 37. Raphael's career can be divided into three phases and styles: his early years in Umbria, a period in Florence where he absorbed artistic traditions, and his final years in Rome working for two popes. Many of his works can be found in the Vatican Palace, including the famous fresco The School of Athens. After his death, Raphael's work had a significant influence on Neoclassical painting, although it was later rejected by groups like the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. Raphael's father was a court painter and Raphael took over the family workshop after his father's death. He trained in the workshop of Perugino and became a fully qualified master by 1500. He worked in various cities in northern Italy before moving to Rome in 1508 to work at the Vatican Palace. He also worked as an architect in Rome and continued to create important works until his death in 1520.

Roman Colosseum

The Colosseum is an oval amphitheater in the center of Rome, Italy, just east of the Roman Forum. Despite its age, it is the largest ancient amphitheater ever built and is still the largest standing amphitheater today. Construction began under the emperor Vespasian (r. 69-79 AD) in 72 and was completed in 80 AD under his successor and heir, Titus (r. 79-81). Further modifications were made during the reign of Domitian. The three emperors that were patrons of the work are known as the Flavian dynasty. The amphitheater was named the Flavian Amphitheatre by later classicists and archaeologists for its association with their family name (Flavius). The Colosseum is built of travertine limestone, tuff (volcanic rock), and brick-faced concrete. It could hold an estimated 50,000 to 80,000 spectators at various points in its history, having an average audience of some 65,000; it was used for gladiatorial contests and public spectacles, including animal hunts, executions, re-enactments of famous battles, and dramas based on Roman mythology, and briefly mock sea battles. The building ceased to be used for entertainment in the early medieval era. It was later reused for housing, workshops, quarters for a religious order, a fortress, a quarry, and a Christian shrine.

Trevi Fountain

The Trevi Fountain (Italian: Fontana di Trevi) is an 18th-century fountain in the Trevi district in Rome, Italy, designed by Italian architect Nicola Salvi and completed by Giuseppe Pannini and several others. Standing 26.3 meters (86 ft) high and 49.15 meters (161.3 ft) wide, it is the largest Baroque fountain in the city and one of the most famous fountains in the world. The fountain has appeared in several films, including Roman Holiday; Three Coins in the Fountain; Federico Fellini's classic, La Dolce Vita; Sabrina Goes to Rome; and The Lizzie McGuire Movie.

Roman Forum

The Roman Forum, also known as the Forum Romanum, is a rectangular plaza surrounded by the ruins of ancient government buildings in Rome. It was initially a marketplace and served as the center of daily life in Rome, with triumphal processions, elections, public speeches, criminal trials, gladiatorial matches, and commercial activities all taking place there. The Forum also had many statues and monuments honoring Rome's notable figures. Today, the Forum is a ruin with intermittent archaeological excavations, attracting millions of tourists yearly. Rome's oldest and most essential structures were located on or near the Forum, including the Regia, the Temple of Vesta, and the Vestal Virgins complex.

Sistine Chapel

The Sistine Chapel is located in the Apostolic Palace in Vatican City and serves as the pope's official residence. It was built by Pope Sixtus IV between 1473 and 1481 and named after him. The chapel is famous for its frescoes, especially the ceiling and The Last Judgment by Michelangelo. It is also the site of the papal conclave, where a new pope is selected. During the reign of Sixtus IV, a team of Renaissance painters, including Sandro Botticelli, Pietro Perugino, Pinturicchio, and Domenico Ghirlandaio, painted frescoes depicting the Life of Moses and the Life of Christ, along with papal portraits and trompe-l'œil drapery. These paintings were completed in 1482, and the chapel was consecrated and dedicated to the Virgin Mary during a ceremony on the Feast of the Assumption. Between 1508 and 1512, Michelangelo painted the chapel's ceiling under the patronage of Pope Julius II. This project, considered one of the significant artistic achievements in human history, changed the course of Western art. Michelangelo also painted The Last Judgment for Popes Clement VII and Paul III between 1535 and 1541. The fame of Michelangelo's paintings has attracted many visitors to the chapel over the past five centuries.

History

The history of Italy covers the ancient period, the Middle Ages, and the modern era. Since classical antiquity, ancient Etruscans, various Italic peoples (such as the Latins, Samnites, and Umbri), Celts, Magna Graecia colonists, and other ancient peoples have inhabited the Italian Peninsula. In antiquity, Italy was the homeland of the Romans and the metropole of the Roman Empire's provinces. Rome was founded as a Kingdom in 753 BC and became a republic in 509 BC when the Roman monarchy was overthrown in favor of a government of the Senate and the People. The Roman Republic then unified Italy at the expense of the peninsula's Etruscans, Celts, and Greek colonists. Rome led Socii, a confederation of the Italic peoples, and later with the rise of Rome, dominated Western Europe, Northern Africa, and the Near East. The Roman Republic saw its fall after the assassination of Julius Caesar. The Roman Empire later dominated Western Europe and the Mediterranean for many centuries, making immeasurable contributions to Western philosophy, science, and art development. After the fall of Rome in AD 476, Italy was fragmented into numerous city-states and regional polities; despite seeing famous personalities from its territory and closely related ones (such as Dante Alighieri, Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Niccolò Machiavelli, Galileo Galilei, and Napoleon Bonaparte) rise, it remained politically divided to a large extent. The maritime republics, particularly Venice and Genoa, rose to great prosperity through shipping, commerce, and banking, acting as Europe's main port of entry for Asian and Near Eastern imported goods and laying the groundwork for capitalism. Central Italy remained under the Papal States, while Southern Italy remained essentially feudal due to a succession of Byzantine, Arab, Norman, Spanish, and Bourbon crowns. The Italian Renaissance spread to the rest of Europe, bringing a renewed interest in humanism, science, exploration, and art with the start of the modern era. Italian explorers (including Marco Polo, Christopher Columbus (Italian: Cristoforo Colombo), and Amerigo Vespucci) discovered new routes to the Far East and the New World, helping to usher in the Age of Discovery, although the Italian states had no occasion to found colonial empires outside of the Mediterranean Basin. By the mid-19th century, the Italian unification by Giuseppe Garibaldi, backed by the Kingdom of Sardinia, led to the establishment of an Italian nation-state. The new Kingdom of Italy, established in 1861, quickly modernized and built a colonial empire, controlling parts of Africa and countries along the Mediterranean. At the same time, Southern Italy remained rural and poor, originating the Italian diaspora. In World War I, Italy completed the unification by acquiring Trento and Trieste and gained a permanent seat in the League of Nations' executive council. Italian nationalists considered World War I a mutilated victory because Italy did not have all the territories promised by the Treaty of London (1915). That sentiment led to the rise of the Fascist dictatorship of Benito Mussolini in 1922. The subsequent participation in World War II with the Axis powers, together with Nazi Germany and the Empire of Japan, ended in military defeat, Mussolini's arrest and escape (aided by the German dictator Adolf Hitler), and the Italian Civil War between the Italian Resistance (aided by the Kingdom, now a co-belligerent of the Allies) and a Nazi-fascist puppet state known as the Italian Social Republic. Following the liberation of Italy, the fall of the Social Republic, and the killing of Mussolini at the hands of the Resistance, the 1946 Italian constitutional referendum abolished the monarchy and became a republic, reinstated democracy, enjoyed an economic miracle, and founded the European Union (Treaty of Rome), NATO, and the Group of Six (later G7 and G20). It remains a strong economic, cultural, military, and political factor in the 21st century.
