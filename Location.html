Geography

Italy is a country located in Southern Europe, also considered part of western Europe.Italy is in the Central European Standard Time Zone, It is bordered by France, Switzerland, Austria, and Slovenia to the north and consists of the Italian Peninsula and the islands of Sicily and Sardinia to the south, as well as many smaller islands. The country's total area is 301,230 square kilometers, with a coastline and border of 7,600 kilometers on the Adriatic, Ionian, and Tyrrhenian seas. It also shares borders with France, Austria, Slovenia, and Switzerland. The enclaves of San Marino and the Vatican City are within Italy, while Campione d'Italia is an Italian exclave in Switzerland. Over 35% of Italy's territory is mountainous, with the Apennine Mountains forming the backbone of the peninsula and the Alps forming most of the northern boundary. Italy is also home to many small volcanic islands and active volcanoes, including Mount Etna, Vulcano, Stromboli, and Vesuvius.

Environment

Italy has a history of environmental problems, but it has made efforts to improve and now ranks 84th worldwide for ecological sustainability. National parks cover about 5% of the country. In contrast, the total area protected by national parks, regional parks, and nature reserves covers about 10.5% of the Italian territory and 12% of the coasts protected by marine protected areas. In the past decade, Italy has become a leading producer of renewable energy, ranking as the fourth-largest holder of installed solar energy capacity and the sixth-largest holder of wind power capacity in 2010. Renewable energies provided approximately 37% of Italy's energy consumption in 2020. However, the country still faces air pollution and traffic congestion issues, particularly in the industrialized north. Italy is also the twelfth-largest carbon dioxide producer and has faced contamination of watercourses and coastal areas due to industrial and agricultural activity. Waste from industrial actions is sometimes not correctly disposed of, leading to health effects on affected communities. Italy operated nuclear reactors until 1990, but the program was terminated after the Chornobyl disaster and a referendum. In 2008, the government attempted to build up to four nuclear power plants with French technology, but a referendum following the Fukushima nuclear accident struck this down. Deforestation, illegal building developments, and poor land management have caused significant erosion and ecological disasters in Italy's mountainous regions. The country had a 2019 Forest Landscape Integrity Index mean score of 3.65/10, ranking it 142nd globally out of 172 countries.

Biodiversity

Italy has a high level of faunal biodiversity, with over 57,000 species recorded, representing more than a third of all European fauna. The country has a diverse climate and habitat due to its location on the Mediterranean Sea and its varied geological structure, including the Alps and the Apennines. Italy is home to 4,777 endemic animal species, including the Sardinian long-eared bat, Sardinian red deer, and the Italian newt. There are also 119 mammal species, 550 bird species, 69 reptile species, 39 amphibian species, 623 fish species, and 56,213 invertebrate species, including 37,303 insect species. Italy has 6,759 recorded vascular plant species, with 1,371 endemic species and subspecies, such as the Sicilian Fir and the Ucriana violet. Italy is a signatory to conservation conventions and directives that protect its fauna and flora, and it has many botanical and historic gardens. The Italian wolf is the national animal, and the strawberry tree is the national tree.

Climate

Italy has a Mediterranean climate, with local variations due to the country's geomorphology and the influence of the Mediterranean Sea. Inland regions have a subtropical to humid continental and oceanic climate, while coastal areas have mild winters and hot, dry summers. The Po valley region has a humid subtropical climate, with cool winters and hot summers. Average winter temperatures range from 0 °C (32 °F) in the Alps to 12 °C (54 °F) in Sicily, and average summer temperatures range from 20 °C (68 °F) to over 25 °C (77 °F). Northern and central regions can experience thunderstorms from spring to autumn.
