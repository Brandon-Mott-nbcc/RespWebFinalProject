Economy & Important Cities/Regions

Central Region

Central Italy encompasses four of the country's 20 regions:

  • Lazio
  • Marches (Marche)
  • Tuscany (Toscana)
  • Umbria

The southernmost and easternmost parts of Lazio (Sora, Cassino, Gaeta, Cittaducale, Formia, and Amatrice districts) are often included in Southern Italy (the so-called Mezzogiorno) for cultural and historical reasons since they were once part of the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies, and southern Italian dialects are spoken. As a geographical region, however, central Italy may also include the areas of Abruzzo and Molise, which are otherwise considered part of Southern Italy for socio-cultural, linguistic, and historical reasons.

Northwest Italy

Northwest Italy (Italian: Italia nord-occidentale or just Nord-ovest) is one of the five official statistical regions of Italy used by the National Institute of Statistics (ISTAT), a first level NUTS region and a European Parliament constituency. Northwest encompasses four of the country's 20 regions:

  • Aosta Valley
  • Liguria
  • Lombardy
  • Piedmont

North East

Northeast Italy (Italian: Italia Nord-Orientale or just Nord-est) is one of Italy's five official statistical regions used by the National Institute of Statistics (ISTAT), a first-level NUTS region, and a European Parliament constituency. The Northeast encompasses four of the country's 20 provinces:

  • Emilia-Romagna
  • Friuli-Venezia Giulia
  • Trentino-Alto Adige/Südtirol
  • Veneto

Culturally and historically, Emilia-Romagna is part of Northwest Italy, but is included in Northeast Italy for statistical reasons.

South Italy

South Italy (Italian: Italia meridionale or just Sud Italia) is one of Italy's five official statistical regions used by the National Institute of Statistics (ISTAT), a first-level NUTS region, and a European Parliament constituency. South Italy encompasses six of the country's 20 regions:

  • Abruzzo
  • Apulia
  • Basilicata
  • Calabria
  • Campania
  • Molise

South Italy is defined only for statistical and electoral purposes. It should not be confused with the Mezzogiorno, or Southern Italy, which refers to the areas of the former Kingdom of the Two Sicilies (once including the southern half of the Italian peninsula and Sicily) with the usual addition of the Western Mediterranean island of Sardinia. The latter and Sicily form a distinct statistical region called Insular Italy.

Insular Italy

Insular Italy (Italian: Italia insulare or just Isole, meaning "islands") is one of the five official statistical regions of Italy used by the National Institute of Statistics (ISTAT), a first level NUTS region and a European Parliament constituency. Insular Italy encompasses two of the country's 20 regions: Sardinia and Sicily. Italy's insular region takes up about one-sixth of the country's total land area. Both of these islands have several smaller islands and archipelagos that are administratively dependent on them. Sicily is the largest island in the Mediterranean Sea, with an area of 25,708 square kilometers, and Sardinia is slightly smaller, with an area of 24,090 square kilometers. The lowlands in this region are generally confined to narrow coastal strips, with the exceptions being the Campidano and Nurra in Sardinia and the Plain of Catania in Sicily, which are 1200 square kilometers and 430 square kilometers, respectively. The majority of the land in this region is hilly, with hills covering about 70% of the territory. Sicily is home to Mount Etna, the highest non-Alpine peak in Italy and the largest active volcano in Europe, while Sardinia is home to the Gennargentu mountain range.

Palmero

Palermo, known locally also as Paliemmu or Palèimmu) is a city in southern Italy, the capital of the autonomous region of Sicily and the Metropolitan City of Palermo, the city's surrounding metropolitan province. The town is noted for its history, culture, architecture, and gastronomy, playing an important role throughout much of its existence; it is over 2,700 years old. Palermo is northwest of the island of Sicily, by the Gulf of Palermo in the Tyrrhenian Sea. Palermo is Sicily's cultural, economic, and tourism capital. It is a city rich in history, culture, art, music, and food. Numerous tourists are attracted to the town for its appealing Mediterranean climate, renowned gastronomy and restaurants, Romanesque, Gothic, Baroque, and Art Nouveau churches, palaces, buildings, and nightlife and music. Palermo is the main Sicilian industrial and commercial center: the main industrial sectors include tourism, services, commerce, and agriculture. Palermo has an international airport and a significant underground economy.[citation needed For cultural, artistic, and economic reasons, Palermo is one of the largest cities in the Mediterranean and is now among the top tourist destinations in both Italy and Europe.

Rome

Rome is the capital and largest city of Italy, located in the central-western part of the country. It is the capital of the Lazio region and the Metropolitan City of Rome, and it is also a special comune called Comune di Roma Capitale. With a population of 2,860,009 people living in an area of 1,285 square kilometers (496.1 square miles), Rome is the most populous comune in Italy and the third most populous city in the European Union within city limits. The Metropolitan City of Rome, which has a population of 4,355,725 people, is the most populous city in Italy, and its metropolitan area is the third largest in the country. Rome is situated along the Tiber River and is known for its seven hills and its nickname as the "Eternal City." It is considered the "cradle of Western civilization and Christian culture" and the center of the Catholic Church. Vatican City, the smallest country in the world, is an independent state located within the city limits of Rome, making it the only example of a country within a city.

Venice

Venice is a city located in northeastern Italy and the capital of the Veneto region. It is built on a group of 118 small islands that are connected by more than 400 bridges and separated by canals. These islands are located in the shallow Venetian Lagoon, an enclosed bay between the mouths of the Po and Piave rivers. In 2020, about 258,685 people lived in Venice, or the Comune di Venezia, with about 55,000 of them residing in the historic island city of Venice (Centro Storico) and the rest living on the mainland (terraferma). Along with the cities of Padua and Treviso, Venice is included in the Padua-Treviso-Venice Metropolitan Area (PATREVE), which has a total population of 2.6 million people. The name Venice comes from the ancient Veneti people who lived in the region in the 10th century BC. The city was the capital of the Republic of Venice for over a millennium, from 697 to 1797. During this time, it was a major financial and maritime power, as well as a center of commerce and art. It also played a role in the Crusades and the Battle of Lepanto. Venice was the first international financial center and reached its peak of prominence in the 14th century. Throughout its history, Venice had many territories along the Adriatic Sea and within the Italian peninsula, which left a lasting impact on its architecture and culture. In 1797, Napoleon ended the city's sovereignty, and in 1866, it became part of the Kingdom of Italy.
